Questions

This notebook uses the record of North Atlantic tropical cyclones kept by the U.S. National Hurricane Center. A row is not a storm. It is one observation of one storm at one moment: where the center of the storm was (lon and lat, in degrees), how fast its winds were blowing (wind, in knots), what Saffir-Simpson category it had reached, and a two-letter status code, where TD is a tropical depression, TS a tropical storm, and HU a hurricane. The remaining status codes describe systems that are not quite tropical. Positions are logged every six hours, so a storm that lasts a week leaves behind about thirty rows. The columns year, month, day, and hour give the time of the observation, and doy gives the day of the year, counting from 1 on the first of January.

  1. Do hurricanes weaken as they travel north? Take the observations from 1990 onward where the status was "HU", add a column giving the wind speed in miles per hour instead of knots (multiply by 1.15078), and plot latitude against that new column.
View code 
(
    storm
    .filter((c.status == "HU") & (c.year >= 1990))
    .with_columns(wind_mph = c.wind * 1.15078)
    .pipe(ggplot, aes(c.lat, c.wind_mph))
    .geom_point()
)

The answer is yes, but the shape is a ceiling rather than a slope. Weak hurricanes turn up at every latitude, so the bottom of the plot stays flat. The top does not. The strongest winds, up around 185 miles per hour, only ever appear between about 12 and 30 degrees, and by 40 degrees nothing in the record has passed 115. Try the plot once more with c.wind in place of c.wind_mph. It is the same picture with different numbers on the axis, which is all that changing a unit should ever do to a graphic.
