17.2 Introduction

Images are among the most ubiquitous forms of data in the modern world. Every day, billions of photographs are captured on smartphones, satellites orbit the Earth generating terabytes of imagery, medical scanners produce detailed views of the human body, and security cameras record countless hours of video. The ability to extract meaningful information from these images has become one of the most important capabilities in data science.

At their core, digital images are simply arrays of numbers. Each pixel in an image stores numerical values representing color or intensity. Yet from these raw numbers emerge faces, objects, scenes, and stories. The challenge of image analysis lies in bridging this gap between low-level pixel values and high-level semantic meaning. How do we go from a grid of numbers to understanding that an image contains a bird, or that a person in a photograph is smiling, or that a tumor is present in a medical scan?

In this chapter, we explore several approaches to extracting information from images. We begin with basic properties that can be computed directly from pixel values, including brightness and color distributions. These simple features, while limited in their semantic power, provide useful descriptors for large collections of images and can reveal interesting patterns in visual datasets. We then turn to embeddings produced by large pre-trained neural networks, which represent each image as a dense vector in the same spirit as the document vectors of Chapter 15. Once images are points in a vector space, the geometric toolkit we developed for text carries over directly: we can map an entire photo collection with UMAP, measure similarities with dot products, and even classify images into categories described in plain English, all without training a model of our own.

The progression in this chapter mirrors the broader evolution of computer vision as a field. Early approaches relied on hand-crafted features computed from pixel statistics. Modern methods use neural networks to learn relevant features directly from data. Both perspectives remain valuable: simple pixel-based features are interpretable, fast to compute, and often sufficient for exploratory analysis, while deep learning models offer unprecedented accuracy for complex recognition tasks. Understanding both approaches equips you to choose the right tool for each analytical situation.

The brightness distribution reflects not just the birds themselves but the entire photographic context.

17.5 Hue, Saturation, and Value

While brightness provides a single summary statistic, the RGB color model used by digital images is not ideal for describing color as humans perceive it. The HSV (Hue, Saturation, Value) color model offers a more intuitive representation that separates color information into three distinct components.

Hue represents the pure color itself, independent of how light or vivid it appears. Hue is measured as an angle around a color wheel, typically ranging from 0° to 360°. Red appears at 0° (and wraps around to 360°), yellow at 60°, green at 120°, cyan at 180°, blue at 240°, and magenta at 300°. This circular representation explains why red and magenta appear adjacent: they are neighbors on the color wheel.

Saturation measures the purity or intensity of a color. A fully saturated color contains no gray: it is vivid and intense. As saturation decreases, the color becomes more washed out, eventually becoming a pure gray at zero saturation. Saturation ranges from 0 (completely desaturated, gray) to 1 (fully saturated, pure color).

Value (also called brightness or lightness in related color models) indicates how light or dark the color is. A value of 0 produces black regardless of hue or saturation, while a value of 1 produces the brightest possible version of that hue and saturation combination.

The mathematical conversion from RGB to HSV proceeds as follows. Given RGB values normalized to the range [0, 1], we first compute:

\[ \begin{aligned} M &= \max(R, G, B) \\ m &= \min(R, G, B) \\ C &= M - m \end{aligned} \]

The value \(M\) is the maximum of the three channels, \(m\) is the minimum, and \(C\) (chroma) measures the range. The HSV components are then calculated as:

\[ V = M \]

\[ S = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } V = 0 \\ \frac{C}{V} & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} \]

\[ H = \begin{cases} 0° & \text{if } C = 0 \\ 60° \times \frac{G - B}{C} \mod 360° & \text{if } M = R \\ 60° \times \left(\frac{B - R}{C} + 2\right) & \text{if } M = G \\ 60° \times \left(\frac{R - G}{C} + 4\right) & \text{if } M = B \end{cases} \]

The value component is simply the maximum RGB value. Saturation measures how much the color differs from gray by comparing the range of RGB values to the maximum. Hue is computed by determining which RGB component is dominant and calculating the position around the color wheel.

In practice we never write any of this out ourselves. The formulas are here so that the three components mean something concrete rather than arriving as three unexplained numbers; OpenCV applies them for us with a single function call, and there is no reason to memorize them.

OpenCV provides built-in functions for color space conversion. Here is how to convert an image from BGR to HSV.

img = cv2.imread(birds.select(c.filepath).row(0)[0])

img_hsv = cv2.cvtColor(img, cv2.COLOR_BGR2HSV)
img_hsv.shape
(224, 224, 3)

The resulting array has the same shape as the original, but now the three channels represent hue, saturation, and value instead of blue, green, and red. Note that OpenCV scales hue to the range [0, 179] (to fit in an 8-bit value while covering the full 360° range) and saturation and value to [0, 255].

17.6 Colors

With images represented in HSV color space, we can classify pixels by their dominant color. The hue value tells us where each pixel falls on the color wheel, allowing us to count how many pixels are predominantly red, orange, yellow, green, and so on.

Our helper method DSImage.compute_colors takes an HSV image and returns a dictionary with the proportion of pixels falling into each color category. It bins the hue values into segments corresponding to common color names and also accounts for the saturation and value (very dark or desaturated pixels are classified separately as black, white, or gray).

17.7 Transfer Learning

The pixel statistics developed so far describe images at the level of raw color. They are useful for exploring a collection, but they fall well short of the questions we usually want to ask. Brightness and color proportions cannot tell us whether a photograph contains a duck or an ostrich, and two photographs of different species taken against similar foliage may have nearly identical color profiles.

We faced a similar gap with text in Chapter 15. There, the solution was to represent each document as a vector and let geometry do the rest: documents with similar vocabulary became nearby points, and distances, similarity scores, and two-dimensional maps of the collection all followed from that representation. We would like the same thing for images: a vector for each image such that pictures with similar content sit close together in space. For text, we could build the vectors ourselves by counting terms and applying TF-IDF weights. For images, no comparably simple recipe exists. Counting pixels by color, as we did above, is the visual analogue of counting words, but nearly all of the meaning in an image lies in how the pixels are arranged, and no small set of hand-written rules describes arrangement well.

The tools that do capture arrangement are deep neural networks: large statistical models with millions of adjustable parameters that are fit (the machine learning term is trained) on enormous collections of labeled images. How these models are built and trained fills entire courses and is beyond the scope of this book. For our purposes, a rough picture is enough. A network trained to classify images processes each picture through a long sequence of stages. Early stages respond to simple patterns such as edges and textures. Later stages combine these into larger structures, such as an eye or a patch of feathers. The final stage converts this accumulated description into a prediction of the image’s category. Just before that last step, the network has reduced the image to a fixed-length list of numbers summarizing its content. That list is an embedding in exactly the sense of Chapter 15: a vector representation in which similar objects are close together.

Training such a network from scratch requires millions of labeled images and weeks of time on specialized hardware, resources far beyond a typical data analysis project. Fortunately, we do not have to train one. Research groups at large companies and universities have trained these models and released them publicly. The final predictions of a released model are usually not what we need, since the categories it was trained on rarely match the ones we care about. But the embedding computed just before the prediction is generic: it describes what is in the image, whatever the image happens to be. Reusing a model trained for one task as the starting point for a different task is called transfer learning, and in one form or another it accounts for the vast majority of practical applications of deep learning.

17.8 Image Embeddings

The Vision Transformer works by dividing an image into a grid of small patches and treating each patch the way a language model treats a word. The patches are then processed through layers that let the model learn relationships between different parts of the image, so the resulting representation captures both local details and global structure.

Calling the model on an image path returns a sequence of 768 numbers: the image’s embedding. Unlike the TF-IDF vectors of Chapter 15, which were sparse and had one interpretable dimension for each term in the vocabulary, these embeddings are dense. Nearly every entry is non-zero, and no individual dimension has a meaning we can name. Only the geometry matters: two images that are visually similar will have embeddings that are close together in the 768-dimensional space, while dissimilar images will have embeddings that are far apart. The precomputed embeddings are stored alongside the rest of the data in a Parquet file. Parquet is a columnar storage format that is particularly well-suited for analytical workloads. Unlike CSV files, Parquet preserves data types exactly, handles nested structures like lists efficiently, and compresses data effectively. This makes it an excellent choice for storing embeddings, which are arrays of floating-point numbers that would be awkward to represent in CSV format.

What can we do with these vectors? Everything we did with document vectors. A natural first step is to visualize the whole collection at once. As in Chapter 15, UMAP can project the 768-dimensional embeddings down to two dimensions while preserving, as much as possible, the relative distances between points. This lets us see how the different bird species arrange themselves in the embedding space.

(

        umap,
        features=[c.vit]
    )
    .predict(full=True)
    .pipe(ggplot, aes(c.dr0, c.dr1))
    .geom_point(aes(color=c.label))
)

Notice that the UMAP projection does an incredibly good job of separating the categories. Images of the same species cluster tightly together, while different species form distinct groups. This is worth pausing over: nothing in our code told the model anything about our ten species. The ViT model was trained on a different set of images with a different set of labels, yet its embeddings capture the visual features that distinguish a robin from a peacock. Compare this with the brightness and color boxplots earlier in the chapter, where the species distributions overlapped heavily. The embedding representation encodes far more of what the images actually show.

17.9 Zero-Shot Classification

The UMAP plot shows that the embeddings separate the species, but it does not, on its own, tell us which cluster is which. To go from visualization to prediction, we will use an even newer class of deep learning models called zero-shot models. These allow us to classify images into categories that we describe in plain language, with no labeled examples and no additional training at all. The idea sounds almost magical at first.

Zero-shot learning became practical with the development of multimodal models that learn to connect images and text in a shared embedding space. These models are trained on massive datasets of image-caption pairs scraped from the internet. By learning to match images with their textual descriptions, the models develop an understanding of concepts that can be expressed in natural language. To classify a new image, we simply compare its embedding to the embeddings of text descriptions of each possible category.

We will use a model called SigLIP (Sigmoid Language-Image Pre-training), which represents the current state of the art in this type of approach. Like ViT, we have wrapped the model in a convenient interface that handles loading and embedding. The image embeddings from SigLIP are also precomputed for us, in the siglip column of our dataset.

The SigLIP model can embed both images and text into the same space. Images that match a textual description will have embeddings that are close together. We measure this closeness with the dot product of the two embedding vectors. Recall from Chapter 15 that the cosine similarity between two unit-length vectors is exactly their dot product; the SigLIP embeddings are scaled to unit length, so the dot product here is the same cosine similarity we used to compare documents. Values close to 1 indicate high similarity, while values close to 0 indicate the vectors are nearly orthogonal (unrelated).

Let’s start with a simple example: we will create a text embedding for “a photo of a canary” and see which images in our dataset are most similar to this description.

embed = (

            siglip.embed_text,

To perform zero-shot classification on the entire dataset, we need to create text embeddings for all of our bird species categories. We can then assign each image to the category whose text embedding it is most similar to. This process requires no training data at all; we are simply leveraging the knowledge the model acquired during its original pre-training.

embed = (
    birds
    .select(text = c.label.unique())
    .with_columns(

            siglip.embed_text, return_dtype=pl.List(pl.Float32)

    )    
)

17.10 Summary

In this chapter, we treated images the way Chapter 15 treated documents: first as raw material for hand-computed summaries, then as points in a vector space. Pixel statistics such as brightness and color proportions are fast to compute and easy to interpret, and they revealed real patterns in the bird photographs, but they describe the entire frame (background, lighting, and photographic style) rather than the subject. Embeddings from pre-trained neural networks close that gap. The ViT model, through transfer learning, converted each photograph into a dense 768-dimensional vector, and a UMAP projection of those vectors separated the ten species almost perfectly, using knowledge the model acquired long before it ever saw our data.

The SigLIP model took the idea one step further by embedding images and text into a single shared space. There, the cosine similarities of Chapter 15 became a classifier: each image was assigned the species name whose text embedding it sat closest to, with no labeled examples and no training on our part. The general lesson is that once complex objects, whether documents or photographs, are represented as vectors, the same small geometric toolkit of distances, similarities, and projections does most of the analytical work.
